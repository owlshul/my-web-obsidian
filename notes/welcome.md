## a. Principles of Evaluation

### What is Evaluation — Recap and Deeper Understanding

You already encountered evaluation briefly in Unit I as the final stage of the policy cycle. Now we go deeper.

Policy evaluation is the systematic, objective assessment of a policy or program to determine whether it worked, how well it worked, at what cost, for whom, and what should happen next. It is the honest answer to the question: **did this policy actually make a difference?**

The word _systematic_ is important. Evaluation is not casual opinion — "this scheme seems to be working" — nor is it political endorsement — "this was a great program." It follows structured methods, uses evidence, and arrives at defensible conclusions.

The word _objective_ is aspirational but important. Evaluation should be insulated from the political pressure to declare programs successful regardless of evidence. In practice, this is hard — governments fund evaluations of their own programs, creating obvious conflicts of interest. But the _norm_ of objectivity is what separates evaluation from propaganda.

---

### Why Evaluation is Often Neglected

Before getting into principles, it's worth understanding why evaluation is the most neglected stage of the policy cycle in most countries including India.

**Political inconvenience**: If an evaluation finds a flagship scheme is failing, it embarrasses the government that designed and implemented it. Politicians have strong incentives to avoid rigorous evaluation or suppress findings.

**Bureaucratic self-interest**: Implementing agencies don't want to be evaluated harshly — it threatens their budgets and reputations. They prefer monitoring systems that count outputs (easy to show progress) over evaluations that measure outcomes (harder to fake).

**Capacity constraints**: Good evaluation requires trained evaluators, data systems, time, and money. Many governments — especially at state and local levels — simply lack these.

**Short political cycles**: Politicians think in election cycles (5 years). Many policy impacts take 10-20 years to materialize. By the time outcomes are visible, the politicians who designed the policy are gone.

**Fear of learning**: Institutionally, many organizations punish failure rather than learning from it. This creates incentives to avoid evaluation that might reveal failure.

Despite all this, evaluation is growing in importance globally — driven by demand for accountability, evidence-based policy movements, and pressure from donors and civil society.

### Core Principles of Evaluation

These principles are the _standards_ that any good evaluation should meet. Think of them as the ethical and methodological code of evaluation practice.

---

#### 1. Utility

The most fundamental principle: **an evaluation is only worth doing if it will actually be used.**

Evaluation consumes significant resources — money, time, expert capacity, data collection effort. If the findings sit in a shelf and nobody reads them, that investment is wasted. So utility demands that evaluation be designed with the _end user_ in mind from the very beginning.

Who will use this evaluation? A finance ministry deciding budget allocation? A program manager trying to improve implementation? A parliamentary committee examining accountability? Civil society monitoring government performance? Each user needs different things — different levels of technical detail, different formats, different timelines.

Utility also demands **timeliness** — an evaluation delivered two years after a program ends, when all decisions have already been made, has zero utility. Findings must reach decision-makers when decisions are still open.

And utility demands **relevance** — the evaluation must answer questions that decision-makers actually care about, not questions that are academically interesting but operationally irrelevant.

---

#### 2. Feasibility

Evaluation must be **realistic and practical** given available resources, time, and access.

The ideal evaluation might require a randomized controlled trial with 10,000 participants, five years of data, and a team of 50 researchers. But if the budget is ₹20 lakhs and the timeline is six months, that ideal is irrelevant. Feasibility demands that evaluation design match real-world constraints.

This doesn't mean settling for bad evaluation — it means choosing the _best possible_ evaluation _given actual constraints_. A well-designed rapid assessment done in three months can be more useful than a technically perfect study that arrives too late.

Feasibility also includes **political feasibility** — will the implementing agency cooperate? Will data be accessible? Will findings be acted upon? An evaluation that triggers such resistance that its findings are suppressed was not feasible even if technically excellent.

---

#### 3. Propriety (Ethics)

Evaluation must be conducted **ethically and with respect for all stakeholders**.

This has several dimensions:

**Informed consent**: People being surveyed or interviewed must know what the evaluation is about and agree to participate. This is especially important when evaluating programs serving vulnerable populations — the poor, children, tribal communities, people with disabilities.

**Confidentiality**: Individual responses must be protected. Evaluation reports should present aggregate findings, not identify individual respondents in ways that could harm them.

**Fairness to stakeholders**: Evaluation findings should fairly represent the perspectives of all stakeholders — not just program managers and government officials, but also beneficiaries, communities, and critics of the program.

**No harm**: The evaluation process itself should not harm participants. Asking trauma survivors to relive difficult experiences without support, or publishing findings that stigmatize communities, violates this principle.

**Transparency about limitations**: Evaluators must honestly acknowledge what their methods can and cannot tell us. Overstating certainty or hiding methodological weaknesses is unethical.

---

#### 4. Accuracy

Evaluation findings must be **technically sound and factually correct**.

This is the methodological principle. Accurate evaluation requires:

- Appropriate research design for the questions being asked
- Valid measurement — measuring what you claim to measure
- Reliable measurement — getting consistent results if the measurement is repeated
- Adequate sample size to support generalizations
- Appropriate statistical methods
- Honest interpretation of what the data shows

Accuracy also demands acknowledgment of **uncertainty**. Evaluation findings are rarely certain — they are probabilistic. An honest evaluation says "this program likely reduced poverty by approximately 15%, with a margin of error of ±5%" rather than falsely claiming precision it doesn't have.

---

#### 5. Independence

The evaluator must be **free from undue influence** by the parties whose program is being evaluated.

This is the hardest principle to achieve in practice. When the government evaluates its own programs (internal evaluation), independence is structurally compromised — the evaluator reports to the same institution whose program is being assessed.

Independence is stronger when:

- External evaluators (independent researchers, audit institutions, NGOs) conduct the assessment
- The evaluator's funding is not controlled by the implementing agency
- The evaluator has contractual protection against suppression of findings
- There are institutional mechanisms for publishing evaluation findings regardless of how positive or negative they are

In India, the **Comptroller and Auditor General (CAG)** is constitutionally independent and conducts performance audits of government programs. CAG reports have exposed failures in numerous schemes — from coal block allocations to MGNREGS implementation. This institutional independence is precisely what makes CAG findings credible.

---

#### 6. Participation

Good evaluation increasingly emphasizes **participation of stakeholders** — especially beneficiaries — in the evaluation process.

Traditional evaluation was extractive: experts came, collected data from people, left, wrote a report for government. Participatory evaluation involves stakeholders in designing the evaluation questions, collecting data, interpreting findings, and using results.

Why participation matters:

- Beneficiaries have knowledge about program functioning that external evaluators will miss
- Participation builds local capacity and ownership
- Participatory findings are more likely to be acted upon because stakeholders feel invested
- It makes evaluation itself an empowerment process — communities learn to assess and demand accountability

The **Social Audit** (discussed in Unit II) is essentially a participatory evaluation mechanism — the community directly assesses whether the program delivered what it promised.

---

#### 7. Systematic Inquiry

Evaluation must follow **structured, documented, replicable methods** — not ad hoc impressions.

This means: clear evaluation questions stated upfront, explicit choice of methods with rationale, documented data collection procedures, transparent analysis, and findings that can be traced back to evidence. Another evaluator following the same methods should arrive at similar conclusions.

This distinguishes evaluation from journalism, political opinion, or casual observation — all of which may reach conclusions about policy programs, but through non-systematic processes.

## b. Methods and Techniques of Evaluation

Now we get into the actual _how_ of evaluation — the specific approaches and tools evaluators use to assess policy effectiveness.

This section is organized from the most fundamental conceptual challenge (the counterfactual problem) through the major methodological approaches.

---

### The Counterfactual Problem — The Core Challenge of Evaluation

Before any method makes sense, you need to understand the central challenge of impact evaluation: **the counterfactual problem**.

Suppose a government runs a skill training program for unemployed youth. After the program, 60% of participants find employment. Does this mean the program worked?

Not necessarily. The question you need to answer is: **what would have happened to these same people if the program had not existed?** Maybe the economy was improving and 55% of similar unemployed youth found jobs even without any training program. In that case, the program only contributed 5 percentage points of improvement — significant but much less than the raw 60% figure suggests. Or maybe the economy was contracting and without the program, only 20% would have found jobs — in which case the program's contribution was enormous (40 percentage points).

The _counterfactual_ is the hypothetical alternative reality where the program didn't happen. You can never directly observe this — you can't run the same policy both with and without simultaneously. This is what makes causal attribution in evaluation so difficult.

All evaluation methods are essentially different strategies for _estimating the counterfactual_ — constructing a credible comparison that approximates what would have happened without the program.

---

### Methods of Evaluation

#### 1. Experimental Evaluation — Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs)

RCTs are considered the **"gold standard"** of impact evaluation because they solve the counterfactual problem most cleanly.

**How it works**: Eligible participants are randomly divided into two groups:

- _Treatment group_: receives the program/intervention
- _Control group_: does not receive the program (or receives an alternative)

Because assignment is _random_, the two groups are statistically equivalent on average before the program begins — same income levels, same education, same age distribution, same everything. Any difference in outcomes after the program can therefore be _causally attributed_ to the program itself, not to pre-existing differences between participants.

**Example**: Abhijit Banerjee and Esther Duflo's **J-PAL** (Abdul Latif Jameel Poverty Action Lab) has conducted hundreds of RCTs evaluating development programs across South Asia and Africa. Their RCT on remedial education in India found that using trained community tutors to teach basic literacy and numeracy to lagging students (Pratham's "Teaching at the Right Level" approach) was dramatically more cost-effective than standard schooling. This finding changed policy in multiple states and internationally.

**Strengths**: Strongest possible causal claims. Controls for all confounding variables simultaneously (both observed and unobserved). Relatively simple to understand and communicate.

**Weaknesses**:

_Ethical problems_: Randomly denying a program to a control group is ethically problematic when the program is something people urgently need (healthcare, food assistance). You cannot randomly assign people to receive or not receive emergency medical treatment.

_External validity_: What works in one context (one village, one state, one country) may not work in another. RCT findings from Kenya don't automatically apply to Bihar.

_Hawthorne effect_: People behave differently when they know they're being studied. Both treatment and control groups may change behavior simply because of the evaluation, distorting results.

_Long-term effects_: RCTs typically run for 1-3 years. Many policy impacts (education, nutrition, infrastructure) take decades to fully materialize.

_Political economy_: RCTs treat programs as technical interventions in isolation, ignoring the political processes that determine whether programs get funded, implemented, and scaled.

---

#### 2. Quasi-Experimental Methods

When true randomization is impossible — which is most of the time in real government programs — evaluators use **quasi-experimental designs** that approximate the counterfactual through statistical methods.

**Difference-in-Differences (DiD)**

DiD compares the _change_ in outcomes for a group that received the program against the _change_ in outcomes for a similar group that didn't, over the same time period.

Simple example: State A implements a new health insurance scheme. State B doesn't. Before the scheme, both states had similar hospitalization rates. After the scheme, State A's hospitalization rate rises by 15 percentage points; State B's rises by 5 percentage points (due to general economic improvement). The DiD estimate is 10 percentage points — the program's effect, net of trends that would have happened anyway.

The key assumption: in the absence of the program, both groups would have followed parallel trends. If State A and State B were already diverging before the program began, the DiD estimate is unreliable.

**Regression Discontinuity Design (RDD)**

Used when a program has a sharp eligibility cutoff. People just above the cutoff (who get the program) are compared to people just below it (who don't). Because people near the cutoff are very similar to each other, the comparison approximates a randomized experiment.

Example: A scholarship is given to students who score above 60% in an exam. Students scoring 61% and students scoring 59% are very similar in ability — the only difference is which side of the cutoff they fall on. Comparing their outcomes years later gives a credible estimate of the scholarship's impact.

**Propensity Score Matching (PSM)**

When no natural cutoff or parallel comparison group exists, PSM statistically constructs a matched control group. For every program participant, PSM finds a non-participant with the most similar observable characteristics (age, income, education, gender, location). The difference in outcomes between matched pairs estimates the program effect.

Limitation: PSM only controls for _observed_ characteristics. If participants and non-participants differ on unobserved factors (motivation, social networks, unrecorded assets), the matching is imperfect and estimates are biased.

---

#### 3. Qualitative Evaluation Methods

Quantitative methods tell you _how much_ a program changed outcomes. Qualitative methods tell you _why_ — the mechanisms, processes, and contextual factors that explain what happened.

**In-depth interviews**: Extended, open-ended conversations with beneficiaries, implementers, and other stakeholders. Explores their experiences, perceptions, and understandings of the program. Captures nuance, context, and unexpected findings that surveys miss.

**Focus group discussions (FGDs)**: Group conversations (typically 6-10 participants) facilitated by a researcher. The group dynamic generates richer discussion than individual interviews — participants respond to each other, challenge each other, and collectively construct meaning. Useful for understanding community-level perceptions of a program.

**Case studies**: Deep examination of a specific unit — a village, a district, a state, an implementing organization — to understand how and why the program worked or failed in that particular context. Case studies don't support statistical generalization but provide rich, contextually grounded understanding.

**Ethnography**: Immersive, long-term fieldwork where the researcher spends extended time within the community being studied, observing and participating in daily life. Most time-intensive but produces the deepest understanding of how programs intersect with people's lived realities.

**Process tracing**: Traces the causal chain between program activities and outcomes step by step, identifying the specific mechanisms through which effects are produced. Useful for testing whether the program's theory of change is actually operating as designed.

---

#### 4. Mixed Methods Evaluation

Most serious evaluations today combine quantitative and qualitative approaches — **mixed methods** — because each compensates for the other's weaknesses.

A rigorous impact evaluation might:

1. Use an RCT or DiD to establish _whether_ the program had an effect and _how large_
2. Use qualitative interviews and case studies to understand _why_ it had that effect and _for whom_
3. Use process evaluation to document _how_ implementation actually happened

The quantitative component provides causal rigor. The qualitative component provides explanatory depth. Together they produce findings that are both credible and actionable.

---

#### 5. Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA)

CBA is an evaluation technique that systematically compares the total costs of a program against its total benefits, converting everything into monetary terms to determine whether the program produces net value for society.

**How it works**:

_Identify all costs_: Direct program costs (budget), indirect costs (staff time, infrastructure), opportunity costs (what else could this money have funded).

_Identify all benefits_: Direct benefits (income gains, health improvements, lives saved), indirect benefits (economic multiplier effects, reduced burden on other services).

_Monetize everything_: This is where CBA gets controversial. How do you put a rupee value on a life saved? On years of education gained? On an ecosystem protected? Economists use techniques like **Value of Statistical Life** (VSL), **willingness to pay** surveys, and **hedonic pricing** to estimate monetary values for non-market goods.

_Calculate Net Present Value (NPV)_: Future costs and benefits are discounted to present value (because a rupee today is worth more than a rupee in ten years). NPV = total discounted benefits minus total discounted costs. Positive NPV means the program is worth doing in economic terms.

_Calculate Benefit-Cost Ratio_: Total benefits divided by total costs. A ratio above 1 means benefits exceed costs.

**Example**: The Copenhagen Consensus (led by Bjorn Lomborg) used CBA to rank global development interventions by cost-effectiveness. Micronutrient supplementation for children consistently ranks extremely high — the economic returns from improved cognitive development and productivity vastly exceed the cost of providing vitamins.

**Strengths**: Forces explicit comparison of alternatives. Makes trade-offs visible and quantifiable. Enables comparisons across very different programs (health vs. roads vs. education).

**Weaknesses**:

_Monetization is contested_: Putting a price on a human life, a community's cultural heritage, or a species' survival is ethically and technically fraught.

_Distribution is ignored_: CBA aggregates costs and benefits across all of society — it doesn't tell you who bears the costs and who gets the benefits. A program with positive NPV might make the rich richer while the poor pay the costs. Cost-benefit analysis is blind to distributional justice.

_Long-term uncertainty_: Discounting future benefits means long-term programs (climate policy, infrastructure) look worse in CBA than short-term ones, even when long-term benefits are enormous.

---

#### 6. Cost-Effectiveness Analysis (CEA)

CEA is a simpler alternative to CBA that avoids the need to monetize outcomes. Instead of asking "are benefits worth the costs in rupees?", CEA asks: "for a given budget, which program achieves the most of a specific outcome?"

Example: You have ₹100 crore to improve child health. Option A reduces child mortality by 500 deaths per year. Option B reduces child mortality by 1,200 deaths per year. Option B is more cost-effective (same budget, more lives saved) — even without putting a rupee value on a child's life.

CEA is widely used in health policy — the **cost per DALY averted** (Disability-Adjusted Life Year) is the standard metric for comparing healthcare interventions globally.

Limitation: CEA can only compare programs pursuing the _same outcome_. You can't use CEA to compare a health program against a roads program — they produce different outcomes that aren't directly comparable.

---

#### 7. Participatory Evaluation

Participatory evaluation (PE) is a philosophy and approach as much as a specific technique. It holds that the people most affected by a program — beneficiaries, communities, frontline workers — should be active partners in the evaluation, not just data sources.

PE emerged from critiques of conventional evaluation as expert-driven, extractive, and disconnected from local realities. It draws on Paulo Freire's pedagogy of the oppressed and participatory action research traditions.

Key participatory techniques:

**Community scorecards**: Facilitated process where community members collectively assess the quality of local services against agreed standards. Used to evaluate schools, health centers, water supply systems. The community generates the scorecard, interprets the findings, and negotiates improvements with service providers.

**Most Significant Change (MSC)**: Beneficiaries and stakeholders narrate stories of significant change — positive or negative — that they believe resulted from the program. Stories are collected, analyzed, and discussed to identify patterns of impact. Particularly useful for programs with complex, hard-to-quantify outcomes (empowerment, behavior change, community cohesion).

**Participatory Rural Appraisal (PRA)**: A family of participatory techniques including social mapping (communities draw maps of their area showing resources, services, and problems), seasonal calendars (documenting how conditions change through the year), and wealth ranking (communities rank households by relative wealth). Used for both program design and evaluation.

---

#### 8. Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E) Systems

In practice, evaluation doesn't happen as a one-off exercise — it is embedded in a continuous **M&E system** that links ongoing monitoring to periodic evaluation.

A well-designed M&E system has:

**Baseline data**: Measurements of key indicators _before_ the program begins. Without a baseline, you cannot measure change. This is one of the most common failures in Indian government programs — schemes launch without collecting baseline data, making subsequent evaluation impossible.

**Regular monitoring data**: Ongoing collection of input, activity, and output data through MIS systems, field visits, and reports.

**Periodic evaluations**: Structured evaluations at defined intervals — midterm evaluation (typically at 50% of program duration) and end-line evaluation (at completion).

**Learning and feedback loops**: Formal mechanisms for feeding evaluation findings back into program management and policy design. Without this, even excellent evaluations produce no change.

India has been gradually strengthening its M&E systems. **NITI Aayog's Development Monitoring and Evaluation Office (DMEO)** is responsible for evaluating centrally sponsored schemes. The **Aspirational Districts Programme** has a real-time data portal tracking 49 indicators across health, education, infrastructure, and finance in 112 most-underdeveloped districts — this is an example of integrated M&E at scale.

---

### Connecting Principles to Methods

A quick way to remember how principles connect to methods:

**Utility** → Choose methods that produce actionable findings for actual decision-makers, on time

**Accuracy** → Choose methods that credibly answer causal questions (RCTs, quasi-experimental where possible; qualitative to explain mechanisms)

**Feasibility** → Match method sophistication to available resources and time

**Propriety** → Ensure ethical treatment of participants in all data collection

**Independence** → Design evaluation governance so findings can't be suppressed

**Participation** → Build in participatory techniques, especially for programs serving marginalized communities

**Systematic inquiry** → Document methods transparently so findings are verifiable
